Engine Technology & Types

Engine Notation Explained

Engine notation identifies both the layout and number of cylinders in an engine. For example, I4 stands for Inline 4-cylinder, meaning four cylinders arranged in a straight row. V8 means eight cylinders arranged in two banks forming a V shape. V12, I6, and V6 follow the same logic — the letter describes the configuration, while the number indicates how many cylinders are present. This notation helps enthusiasts quickly understand performance potential, smoothness, and engine character.

How an Engine Works

Internal combustion engines operate through a four-stroke cycle: intake, compression, combustion, and exhaust. During intake, air and fuel enter the cylinder. Compression increases pressure and prepares the mixture for ignition. A spark plug ignites the mixture, causing combustion that forces the piston downward. This linear motion rotates the crankshaft, converting chemical energy into mechanical power that drives the wheels. Electric motors operate differently by converting electrical energy directly into rotational motion using magnetic fields.

Types of Engines

Inline Engine

Inline engines have cylinders arranged in a straight line. They are simple in design, cost-effective to manufacture, and widely used in everyday vehicles. Inline-4 engines are common in compact cars, while Inline-6 engines are known for their smooth operation and balanced performance.

Examples: Toyota Supra MK4 (I6), BMW 3 Series, Honda Civic, Nissan Skyline GT-R R34, Mazda MX-5

V Engine

V engines arrange cylinders in two angled banks forming a V shape. This configuration allows more cylinders in a compact space, making it ideal for high-performance vehicles. V6 engines balance efficiency and power, while V8, V10, and V12 engines are known for strong acceleration and deep exhaust notes.

Examples: Ford Mustang GT, Chevrolet Camaro, Ferrari 488 GTB, Lamborghini Huracán, Mercedes-AMG C63

Boxer Engine

Boxer engines use horizontally opposed cylinders that move outward like boxers throwing punches. This layout lowers the center of gravity, improving stability and handling. It is commonly used by brands focused on balanced driving dynamics.

Examples: Subaru WRX STI, Subaru BRZ, Porsche 911, Porsche Cayman, Toyota 86

Rotary Engine

Rotary engines replace pistons with a spinning triangular rotor. They are compact, lightweight, and capable of very high RPM. Although less common due to fuel efficiency challenges, they are loved for their smooth power delivery and unique sound.

Examples: Mazda RX-7, Mazda RX-8, Mazda Cosmo, Mazda RX-3, Mazda RX-2

Electric Motor

Electric motors use electromagnetic fields to produce rotation. They provide instant torque, smooth acceleration, and high efficiency. Unlike combustion engines, they have fewer moving parts and require less maintenance.

Examples: Tesla Model S, Porsche Taycan, Rimac Nevera, Nissan Leaf, BMW i4

Hybrid Engine

Hybrid engines combine a traditional internal combustion engine with one or more electric motors. They improve fuel economy while maintaining strong performance. Hybrid systems can switch between electric and combustion power or use both simultaneously.

Examples: Toyota Prius, McLaren P1, Ferrari LaFerrari, Porsche 918 Spyder, Honda Accord Hybrid
